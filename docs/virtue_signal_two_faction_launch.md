# VIRTUE SIGNAL: Two-Faction Launch

## The Two Arcs

**REFORM/RESTORE (Restorationist):** Right-wing populist. Working-class Elephant & Castle. Tommy Lemon. Margaret. Legitimate grievance, ugly conclusions. The player watches their ideology crack when it meets Yusuf's face.

**ANTIFASCIST (Liberator):** Far-left revolutionary. Trust-fund Brixton. The player dismantles the system and discovers they have no plan for what comes next. Revolution without responsibility is a tantrum.

These are the two most fun, most action-heavy, most politically opposed arcs. A player who does both sees the same city from opposite ends. The same events look different from each side. The same NPCs are allies in one arc and enemies in the other.

## The Centrist Announcement

When the game launches, the roadmap says:

> **Coming Soon: The Centrist Arc**
> "We tried to build it. Nothing happened. We submitted a planning application for the storyline and it's been under review for 6 months. A committee has been formed to assess whether the committee that reviews the storyline is fit for purpose. In the meantime, we've been told to manage expectations. We expect the Centrist Arc to arrive in due course. Your patience has been noted."

That's the announcement. That IS the Centrist content until the DLC ships.

### The Barrage Arc (shared, 12 missions)

Both factions investigate Barrage. The evidence trail is the same. The player's reaction to the evidence is different:

- **Restorationist:** Devastation. Barrage was their champion. The controlled opposition reveal is personal.
- **Liberator:** Vindication. They always knew. "I told you he was a plant." But the reveal is less satisfying because they never believed in him, so losing him costs nothing. The Liberator player learns that being right isn't the same as winning.

## LIBERATOR ARC (new, designed now)

### The Character Arc

**Fixed idea:** "The system must be destroyed."

**Broken by:** The discovery that destruction without construction is just chaos. The Liberators are brilliant at tearing things down. They have no idea what to build. The revolution succeeds and the morning after is the terrifying part.

**Greene's compromise:** The player is a trust-fund revolutionary. Not a hypocrite exactly, just someone whose radicalism has never been tested by real consequences. They quote theory. They attend protests. They live in a house their parents own. The game slowly strips away each layer of comfort until the player is genuinely on the line, and then asks: are you still a revolutionary when it costs you everything, not just your Saturday afternoon?

**Dostoevsky's fixed idea:** "The system is evil and must be destroyed" is approximately 60% correct. The 40% that's wrong: there's nothing to replace it with, and the people who'll suffer most in the chaos are the ones the Liberators claim to be fighting for.

### Key Liberator Characters

**Tarquin Wells:** The player's main contact. 26. Lives in a Brixton townhouse his parents bought in 2008 for £340,000 (now worth £1.2m). Masters in Critical Theory from Goldsmiths. Never had a full-time job. Organises direct actions with military precision and genuine tactical intelligence. His weakness: he's never been cold, hungry, or truly afraid. His revolution is an intellectual exercise. When it becomes real, he'll discover whether he's a revolutionary or a graduate student with a megaphone.

**Suki Okafor:** 30. Nigerian-British. Actually grew up poor. Actually experienced the system's worst. Joined the Liberators because nobody else was fighting. Quietly furious at the trust-fund kids who run the movement. She doesn't say this. She just does twice the work and gets half the credit. She's the most competent person in the faction and she knows the revolution will eat itself. She stays because the alternative is doing nothing.

**Professor Harrington:** 58. Teaches at the university in the Composter district. Wrote the books the Liberators quote. Has tenure, a pension, and a house in Dulwich. He gives fiery lectures about dismantling capitalism and then drives home in a Volvo to a four-bedroom detached. He's not a fraud. He genuinely believes. But his beliefs have never cost him anything. The player will eventually ask him to sacrifice something real. His response reveals everything.

### The Chapel in the Liberator Arc

The Liberator player's relationship with the chapel is the most combative. They don't want to be here. The Church represents everything they oppose: hierarchy, tradition, patriarchy, conservatism.

But it heals.

**Act 2:** The player avoids the chapel. They use other healing methods (the Liberators have a first-aid station at the squat). But the first-aid station only heals 50%. The chapel heals 100%. The game is mechanically pushing them toward the building they don't want to enter.

**Act 3:** The player enters the chapel. Arms crossed. Father Brennan doesn't comment on their body language. He doesn't evangelise. He nods. "Sit where you like." The player heals. They leave. They feel something they don't want to examine.

**Act 3b:** Yusuf is at the chapel. The Liberator player has been defending asylum seekers as a political position. Now there's an actual asylum seeker in front of them. He's Catholic. He's socially conservative. He doesn't agree with the Liberators about most things. He agrees with them about one thing: people should be free. The Liberator player expected a grateful victim. They got a complicated person.

**Act 4:** Margaret is at the chapel. The Liberator player has been fighting against people like Margaret. She's a Restorationist. She says things that make the player's skin crawl. But she's also a 73-year-old widow sitting alone in a cold church. And her husband was Jamaican. And her children don't visit. The Liberator player's categories don't accommodate Margaret. She's the enemy who married across racial lines decades before the Liberators were born. Their ideology says she's a racist. Their eyes say she's a lonely old woman.

**Father Brennan to the Liberator player (one line, late game):**

> "You want to burn it all down. I understand the impulse. But this building has been here since 1887. It survived the Blitz. It survived the Civility Index. It'll survive you too. That's not a challenge. It's a comfort. Some things outlast the things that try to destroy them. Including the things that need destroying."

### The Liberator Ending

The revolution succeeds. The Civility Index infrastructure is physically destroyed. Cameras torn down. Compliance centres burned. The government falls.

**Week 1:** Euphoria. Dancing in the streets. People say whatever they want. The cameras are dark.

**Week 2:** Factions turn on each other. Restorationists take Elephant & Castle. Composters barricade Shoreditch. Old Guard hire private security and wall off Kensington. Landanstan becomes a patchwork of territories.

**Month 1:** The Liberators have no plan. Tarquin is in meetings that produce nothing. Suki is exhausted. The player is doing logistics: who collects the bins? Who fixes the water mains? Who mediates when two households in the same street disagree about noise?

**Month 3:** Each territory has independently recreated the Civility Index under a different name. The Restorationists call theirs "Patriotism Score." The Composters call theirs "Inclusion Rating." The Liberators, who destroyed the original, have created a "Revolutionary Commitment Assessment" to identify counter-revolutionaries.

**The final scene:** A Liberator official sits at a desk in the ruins of the old Department for Community Safety. They're processing reports. Citizens flagged for "insufficient revolutionary commitment." The official stamps a form: "Compulsory Solidarity Workshop."

They look up. They see the player. They say: "Don't look at me like that. Someone has to keep things running."

**Tarquin's fate:** He's on the community council. He's not bad at it. He's just... a bureaucrat now. He uses phrases like "we need to be strategic about resource allocation." He catches himself. He hears Harmer in his own voice. He carries on because there's nothing else to do.

**Suki's fate:** She left. Went to Birmingham. Got a job. A real job. She was the most committed revolutionary and the first to walk away because she was the only one who understood that what they built wasn't what they fought for.

**Professor Harrington's fate:** Still teaching. Still writing about revolution. Still driving home in a Volvo. Nothing changed for him. Nothing ever does.

- The pub (one interior)

- Father Brennan (one character model, two sets of 15 lines)
- Margaret, Yusuf, Carol, Harmer, Barrage, Pressmore (shared NPCs)
- The 5 shared missions (S1-S5)

### What's Built Per Arc

**Restorationist-only:** Tommy's missions (R1-R2), Margaret's flat, the occupation chain mechanic in R9, the Aftermath environmental changes, Restorationist ending sequence.

**Liberator-only:** The squat interior, the barricade mechanic (L9), the crane climb (L4), Tarquin/Suki/Harrington (3 character models + dialogue), the Morning After environmental state, Liberator ending sequence.

### The Big Saves

- **The Rally/Counter-Rally (R5/L2):** Same event. Same location. Same crowd. Same chaos. Different player position. Build the riot ONCE. Both arcs use it.
- **The Hotel (R8/R9/L7):** One building. Three missions. Restorationists inspect it, then occupy it. Liberators defend it. All using the same interior.
- **The Gym:** One mission. Both arcs.
- **The Ambulance:** Same mechanic, different passenger audio. One driving route. Two sets of dialogue.
- **The Chapel:** One interior for the entire game. NPC swaps per act per faction.
